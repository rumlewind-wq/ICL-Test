# 1. [[Introduction to International Commercial Law]]

## Core questions (source: [[ICL emne fra bøger]])
- Definition and subject-matter of ICL
- EU involvement in cross-border civil justice
- Sources of ICL: regulations, conventions, model laws, arbitration rules
- Interaction between domestic law and PIL frameworks
- Architecture of cross-border dispute settlement: litigation + arbitration
- Role of free movement principles in EU civil justice
- Foundations of contractual PIL + tort PIL
- Overview of international commercial dispute mechanisms
- Primary sources: Lecture 1 (Calster ch. 1; Moses ch. 1) and the course description covering litigation, arbitration, and choice of law

## Instrument map (source: [[Bestemmelseoversigt ICL]])
- **EU Private International Law Instruments (Jurisdiction & Judgments)**: [[Brussels I Recast Regulation (EU) 1215.2012]], [[Brussels I Regulation 44/2001]], [[Brussels Convention 1968]], [[Lugano Convention 2007]], [[Lugano Convention 1988]]
- **EU Choice-of-Law Instruments (Applicable Law)**: [[Rome I Regulation 593/2008]], [[Rome II Regulation 864/2007]], [[Rome Convention 1980]]
- **Hague Conference Instruments (Global Litigation)**: [[Hague Choice of Court Convention 2005]], [[Hague Judgments Convention 2019]], [[Hague Convention 1955 on Sales]]
- **International Arbitration Framework**: [[UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration]], [[UNCITRAL Arbitration Rules]], [[New York Convention 1958]], [[UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Commerce 1996]]
- **International Substantive Law**: [[CISG 1980 – Sale of Goods]]

## Lecture roadmap (source: [[ICL topics som slettes delvist når lavet]])
- Lecture 1: EU litigation overview, global litigation overview, international commercial arbitration overview, UNCITRAL framework, New York Convention, Brussels I Regulation, Hague Judgments Conventions (2005/2019), international contracts overview, PIL fundamentals, interaction between litigation and arbitration, Model Law vs. arbitration rules, territorial vs. extraterritorial application, and instrument hierarchy
- Lecture 2: Scope of application for Brussels I / Brussels Ia, civil and commercial matters, general jurisdiction (defendant’s domicile), residual jurisdiction, special jurisdiction for contracts and torts, habitual residence as a connecting factor, place of performance, place of the harmful event, and multi-party jurisdiction mechanics
- Lecture 3: Choice of court agreements, prorogation, jurisdiction by appearance, insurance / consumer / employment jurisdiction rules, loss of jurisdiction, asymmetric clauses, forum non conveniens rejection, and protective jurisdiction regimes
- Lecture 4: Recognition and enforcement of judgments, grounds for refusal, public policy exception, procedural guarantees, circulation of judgments, prohibition of substantive review, authentic instruments, court settlements, provisional measures, Article 45 automatic recognition, and limits on reviewing foreign jurisdiction
- Lectures 5–6: Rome Convention and Rome I Regulation topics covering party autonomy, scope, default rules, characteristic performance, escape clause, formal and material validity, capacity, consumer/employment/insurance contracts, assignment and subrogation, overriding mandatory rules, and interaction with Brussels I
- Lecture 7: [[Hague Choice of Court Convention 2005]] and [[Hague Judgments Convention 2019]] coverage, including exclusive choice-of-court agreements, indirect jurisdiction filters, recognition/enforcement regimes, acceptable bases, refusal grounds, and the package approach between the 2005 and 2019 conventions
- Lectures 8–11: International commercial arbitration structure (consent, institutions, seat theory), arbitration agreements (writing, separability, arbitrability, non-signatories), procedure and applicable law (lex arbitri, party autonomy, interim measures, evidence), tribunal management (constitution, competence-competence, case management), awards (form, reasoning, remedies, costs), annulment (Model Law art. 34 grounds, partial annulment, public policy), and recognition/enforcement under the [[New York Convention]]
